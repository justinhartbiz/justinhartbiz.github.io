The Voynich Manuscript

A 600-year-old mystery that has defeated cryptographers for centuries

The Enigma

Dating from approximately 1404-1438, the Voynich Manuscript is a handwritten codex that has confounded experts for over a century. Written in an unknown writing system and filled with strange illustrations of plants, astronomical diagrams, and bizarre scenes, the manuscript was discovered in 1912 by Polish book dealer Wilfrid Voynich.

Currently housed at Yale University's Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library (cataloged as MS 408), the manuscript has resisted all attempts at decipherment by the world's top cryptographers and linguists. Despite its indecipherable nature, statistical analyses reveal the text exhibits real linguistic properties that distinguish it from random gibberish.

Timeline of Decoding Attempts

Over the centuries, many scholars and cryptographers have attempted to decode the manuscript, all without success.

1921

William Newbold

Claimed to have decoded the manuscript as Roger Bacon's scientific writings. His method was later discredited as overly complex pattern-seeking.

1944

Hugh O'Neill

Identified certain plants as New World species, suggesting the manuscript might post-date Columbus's voyages.

1978

John Stojko

Proposed the text was written in an Ukrainian dialect using a unique writing system. His translation described battles and political events.

1995-2000

Computer Analysis Era

Statistical analyses by various researchers identified linguistic patterns consistent with natural languages, challenging hoax theories.

2014

Stephen Bax

Claimed to have deciphered 14 characters and some words, focusing on labels of illustrated plants. His work has not led to a full translation.

2019

Gerard Cheshire

Proposed the manuscript was written in proto-Romance language. Most scholars have dismissed this theory as unconvincing.
